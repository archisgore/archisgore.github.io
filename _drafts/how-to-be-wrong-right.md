A famous line. An oft-repeated phrase. The subject of dozens of TED talks, hundreds of self-help books, and thousands of blogs and gurus around the world.

Don’t be afraid to fail. Don’t fear rejection. We hear this every day.

And yet…. we don’t quite live it. Neither do the bloggers. Neither do the talkers. **You *should* fear rejection and failure.** The instinct is given to us for a purpose, and as a species, we’ve done quite well for ourselves.

So what’s really up? Should we fear failure or not?

We should always fear failure, and we should always seek to avoid it, so long as we acknowledge we can never get rid of it. The point is to accept failure will happen no matter how hard you try to avoid it, and to fail in non-obvious and new ways, and to learn something from it.

It is also a myth that all failure has something to teach. It does not. Well… technically what you might always learn is, “Doing it for the 15th time still yielded no change in outcome.”

So how do you fail right? How do you learn? How to be wrong in the right way? How to fail in the right way?

Nothing of what I’m talking about is new. In fact most of modern “AI” and “Deep Learning” are a set of very simple rules and methods — almost similar to what I’m about to describe.

### Begin with a Model

Almost everyone who is indecisive and risk-averse have one thing in common. They lack a model. They compensate for lack of a model by adding MORE facts. More information. It still leads them nowhere, so they keep broadening the scope of information/facts way past the strenuous realms of relevance.

So what exactly is a model? Why does it help?

A Model has two parts:

1. It has at least ONE dimension on which you can gauge “better” or “worse”. Let’s call it a… “line”. Just a plain line. Towards the right is better. Towards the left is worse.
2. A CHOICE to change where you can fall on the line.
