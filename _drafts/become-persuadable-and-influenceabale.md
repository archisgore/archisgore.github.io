The world is full of people practicing the dark arts [of influencing people](https://www.amazon.com/Influence-Psychology-Persuasion-Robert-Cialdini/dp/006124189X) or [changing minds](https://www.amazon.com/How-Change-Minds-Influence-Manipulation-ebook/dp/B00CKVRCYY/ref=sr_1_3?crid=DN35OSX8FX33&keywords=how+to+change+minds&qid=1646171629&s=books&sprefix=how+to+change+minds%2Cstripbooks%2C133&sr=1-3). When I started at Polyverse, these were but two of the many books I was recommended, as every new startup is wont to get.

I have since learned that there really is no art to influencing or persuading people. The lack-of-secret to influencing people is as stupidly simple as it is impossible to influence or persuade anyone to believe it: be truthful, be honest, be vulnerable, and be authentic.

After years of failed sales goals, I learned to connect to customers and close sales better than salespeople who were following TED-talk-worthy techniques. I learned to walk into extremely hostile customer calls and have them walk away being winners.

Far too many meetings are wasted with everyone trying to influence and persuade other people. To the extend that some meetings are insufferably cliche-laden — people agreeing with each other in a misunderstood application of “[yes, and…](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yes,_and...)”, or someone [awkwardly praising you](https://www.gvsu.edu/hro/the-art-of-giving-feedback-and-compliments-193.htm) in the middle of a conversation.

Far few meetings have people who are there to be persuaded and influenced. If you have incessant meetings being set up repeatedly without progress, the problem may not be [the lack of writing culture](https://thehustle.co/02162021-amazon-writing/), or that it could have been an email, but rather that everyone is simultaneously trying to, and failing at, being persuasive or influential.

Over the past couple of years, I’ve increasingly often learned something useful, and consequently changed my mind during meetings.
