This is a rant, thought-experiment and a fun little side-project I’m working on.

#### The Pantone Color Language

If I ask you to picture “A blue mug” what do you see? Was it this?

![](/assets/images/winetone-a-pantone-for-wine/image-5a584f291ae1.bin)

Or this?

![](/assets/images/winetone-a-pantone-for-wine/0_RYbh37yFAVLP0LiQ.png)

Using words alone, how would you convey to the next person the precise blueness of the mug. Sure we could say “light blue” or “dark blue” and we’d still be wrong most of the time.

There is of course a sure-fire way to convey a color — by sharing the color’s spectral analysis. But color spectrum alone captures but dimension of the color’s perception. Is it glossy or matte? Does it have texture? Think of the difference between a [musical chord, a musical note and a frequency tone.](https://www.learncolorpiano.com/notes-vs-chords-whats-the-difference/)

Say we produced a set of labels to capture all this information. How could you tell someone to reproduce that precise perception of color anywhere in the world, using words alone? To be consistently rendered on a variety of media — what about subtractive vs additive colors? What about the pigments to be used?

This is the problem the Pantone Color System solves.

#### Purpose of Language

Language exists to convey an idea from one person to another. A language fails, even if it is perfect, if it fails at the task of conveying what needs to be conveyed. Without meaning, a language is pointless.

Every Language itself is far from perfect.
