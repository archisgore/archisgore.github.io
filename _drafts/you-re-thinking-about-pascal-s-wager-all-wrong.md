There was no chance

[Pascal’s Wager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_wager) is the idea that: believing in the existence of God, if later proven true, has an infinite reward, but even if proven false, requires only the small price of belief. So why wouldn’t you just believe in God just in case?

We continually face (what we think are) these wagers, from Gambling to MLMs and Ponzi schemes.

I won’t rehash the ad-nauseum philosophical [critiques of the wager](https://www3.nd.edu/~jspeaks/courses/mcgill/201/pascal-wager.html). I do want to talk about how we misunderstand the meaning of, “there’s a chance”.

Let’s get the easy low-hanging fruit out of the way on why none of these are really Pascal’s Wagers:

1. Pascal’s Wager provides a uniformly infinite payoff to everyone who believes. Being the first to believe or having more/harder belief, doesn’t change the payoff. Does your infinite-return investment opportunity pay back the same whether you put in a penny or a pound?
2. Pascal’s Wager is requires no repetition. You never have to try twice. Does your infinite-return investment require you to continue to invest more?

With that out of the way, do you really have a chance at winning? How do you know?

At the simplest level, for there to be a chance of something happening, it must meet one simple criteria: That there is no contra-indicator to your desired outcome happening.

Put simply, if you were to work backwards from the outcome you are hoping for (some big large payout, gambling win, lottery win, etc.), all the way back to when you make the bet, you must know conclusively that there is nothing that’s going to prevent the outcome.

That sounds like a low bar. Shouldn’t we be focusing on all the ways we can win? By building a bigger sales network under us, because of our personal charm that separates us? Or the empirical evidence of the big talker on stage driving a Lambo?

Take gambling for example. Even the ridiculously lopsided bias towards the house only exists [because it is regulated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cereus_Poker_Network%5C). For there to even be a chance that your bet on 35 black in Roulette ever wins, there have to be no loaded balls, rigged table, or magnets, that actively prevent it from ever happening at all.

**Using contra-positives effectively**

If I could go back to my formal logic class and emphasize the one decision making tool I would use daily, [it would be contra-positives](https://www.thoughtco.com/converse-contrapositive-and-inverse-3126458).

While we’re all told that success isn’t guaranteed, and we all need to take chances, and such, we are never taught to think about all the things that need to happen, for there to be a random chance at all.

There’s a reason that even gambling, despicable as it may be, has to be regulated, because when it’s not, you go from having a comically bad chance of winning, to [absolutely no chance of winning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cereus_Poker_Network).
