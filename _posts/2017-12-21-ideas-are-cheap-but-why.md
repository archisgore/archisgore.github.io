I wrote this up as an internal deterrent on how to ensure we’re talking about the right thing. I think this applies to everyone though. Emotions are powerful tools — they inspire us to do impossible things, and they can also blind us to things that might be impossible.

When I think about any idea, I run them through two major sniff tests — is it obvious, and is it a deux ex machina?

### Is it obvious?

If we were a baseball team, and we were constantly losing, is your problem statement to “score more runs”? Please test this hypothesis before submitting an idea. Instead, submit ideas on how to score more runs, or what problems are holding back scoring of runs, and so on.

**Examples**: “*We should score more runs*”, “*We strike more batters out*”, “*We should make it EVEN simpler*”, “*We should have more sales*”, “*We should protect our customers*”, “*We should win customers hearts*”.

Obvious ideas don’t help because… they state what is not up for debate. It is very tempting to wish a solution into existence by stating the importance of the problem.

Passion. Willpower. Emotion. Walking into a room, and taking off cool aviator shades and slamming your hand on a table and exclaiming, “We have to score more runs damnit!” All these things emphasize more and more what is already conceded.

It is okay to feel helpless. We can fix that. Restating just how important something is, gets us no closer to the solution.

You might wake up every morning wondering why people don’t do the obvious? We need less traffic in Seattle! There. I stated the problem. Do you want me to tell you how important it is? Why aren’t you solving it then?

### Is it a Deus ex Machina?

<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deus_ex_machina>

DEMs, for short, are wish-fulfillment devices, and exceptionally tempting when times get tough and there are no easy solutions. You take all your problems and assign them to a black-box magical device. You then wish this device into existence.

Examples are: “*I know how we solve the energy crisis, a car that is fuelled by water!*”

1. The idea as stated is sound.
2. The idea will literally make 1 trillion dollars.
3. Everyone will buy it.
4. It is clear, precise, concise and accurately described.
5. It is impossible to build. The law of conservation of energy prevents it. The fundamental fabric of physical existence prevents it.

We should be wary of DEMs and quickly identify them when they slip in our midst. Not because they don’t make sense, but rather because they DO! It is easy to get caught up in an emotional and psychological “What if” loop. “But what if we just built this instead? It would totally solve ALL problems!” They can lead to a dangerous echo-chamber.

Prototyping DEMs requires a lot of discipline. They can easily trigger the sunk cost fallacy — “But we’ve invested so much time in this. If we only follow through, we’ll make 1 trillion dollars. What’s the loss of an additional 20 million for that high payoff?”

### Sniff test you’re thinking of a Deus ex Machina

There is a very easy and reliable test to rule out DEMs: Is your imagination focussed on ***building* the DEM** or ***using* the DEM**?

If you’re in the former, it’s a legit proposal. If you’re in the latter, it’s a DEM. DEMs have MANY uses. That’s why they are dangerous discussion and brainstorming topics. But you’re not really brainstorming. You’re imagining the applications of a DEM. Your brain is telling you you’re solving the problem. You aren’t.

Even though your mind convinced you otherwise, post facto, remember that you formulated the DEM to fit those requirements. You went from a sales pitch to the DEM.

When presenting though, you assumed the DEM exists and gave the sales pitch presupposing its existence. It can feel like personal rejection when the DEM is rejected because the pitch made PERFECT SENSE! How can someone reject a 1 trillion dollar proposal? What fiscally irresponsible person does that?

This is your mind playing tricks on you. Don’t give in.

We almost never start with “I have an internal combustion engine that uses water and provides usable mechanical energy. I wonder what we could do with it.”

We almost always start with, “I wish I had a source of cheap mechanical energy. I wonder how we might get that…. oh I know! Water is cheap. Let’s use that instead of gasoline!”
