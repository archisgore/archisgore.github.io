As I work with people who were born after I started working in tech, my turn to tell you one thing we had “back in my day” culturally that I think is our industry is missing. Perhaps it was so subtle, pervasive or simple that it doesn’t make into articles about the future of Engineering.

Today’s rant is about why AI isn’t the reason we don’t have a healthy Junior Dev pipeline, the same way NodeJS didn’t stop a pipeline of engineers learning C/C++/Rust/Assembly/CUDA. The reason is far simpler to fix and uncomfortable to admit.

Want to know what we had 20 years ago that this generation doesn’t get?

#### We had a lot of “eff around time”

My career started towards the end of the software cambrian explosion when every week we had new operating systems, new processor architectures, new motherboards, RPC frameworks, REST vs RPC debates and so much.

But the absolute key point is — I spent my first five years of my career shipping absolutely nothing useful. I worked on SOAP, REST, distributed filesystems, Desktop-in-the-Browser, goofy OSes, and so much more. And a lot of that diverse exposure helps me out today.

There’s a myth we believe despite all of human history stating otherwise: That humans don’t learn or innovate unless we force them to. Which is directly contradicted by everything from the first smallpox vaccine before germ theory, to Mendel’s invention of statistics and discovery of genes, to Joseph Fourier’s then-controversial infinite-sum-of-waves during WW2, all point to people who will simply not be satisfied or contained with the status quo. What’s more telling is these were the people who survived, which means there probably were 100 times as many who tried and failed.

All the Senior/Principal engineers we know of partly grew because they moved up the junior ladder, but what they really grew from was just an incessant amount of effing around and making mistakes.

Everyone I know who understands how a MOSFET works or how to produce build an npn transistor, or build a logic gate, doesn’t know it because there was a junior engineer task assigned to them. They know it because it was fun to know. What being a junior used to mean is that we’d tolerate a significant amount of people building logic gates for a few months even when there wasn’t anything innovative in doing so, and no direct billable benefit to it. Because we knew that having someone on the bench who could build logic gates and an entire processor through one PDF bug can break iMessage with zero-clicks: <https://cybersecuritynews.com/imessage-0-click-exploit-iphone-users/>

I understand the appeal of “Frameworks”. Wall street and the industry just loves frameworks and systems and branding because simplification doesn’t sell.

What bothers me further with the Frameworks for developing Junior Developers is it indexes so much on Code Smells and Lines of Code Coverage or building an intuition for Async protocols and deadlocks. Mentorship is at best 2nd-hand learning — you’re going to get a cult that parrots dogma, not problem solvers grounded in first-principles.

What bothers me even more is it misses the biggest aspect of seniority — the ability to collaborate, to move conversations forward, to take difficult feedback, to miss deadlines — a LOT, to miss goals — a LOT, to deal with a difficult coworker, a difficult boss, a hostile company, and so much more. Almost anyone who’s credibly valued in the industry has caused a cataclysmic event in their company — I’ve brought down at least: live.com, amazon.com, bits of facebook.com and for healthy measure I’m pretty sure I’ve hurt instagram and threads a LOT. The key growth skill is being able to show up the next day, face your coworkers, own up to your accountability and do better. That’s what AI might really take away.

What we really truly need to develop this generation of Junior Devs is to give them lots and lots of eff-around time, exposure, opportunity and feedback. I promise, AI or otherwise, everyone wants to build compilers, filesystems, MOSFETs, logic gates. We have to give them the room to do it, and fail a lot, and learn how to handle that failure.

Kids aren’t going to stop learning because of AI. They’re going to stop learning because we’ve disincentivized them from learning.
