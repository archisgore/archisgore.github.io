You can skip this if you’re a proficient cook — you intrinsically do this, but don’t consciously recognize that you do.

I spent most of this past year teaching baking to donors to my favorite [art gallery](https://www.cocaseattle.org/) in Seattle. I write this in retrospect knowing that:

- Very few, if any, cookbooks, blogs or videos really teach what daily cooking is like (at home or even in a commercial kitchen.)
- If they do, they’re not known enough that it continues to intimidate people.

So let’s get to it.

#### Is a recipe, really a recipe?

I’ll demonstrate my point with the [Simplest Cake Recipe Ever](https://www.google.com/search?channel=frsc&client=firefox-b-1-d&q=simplest+cake+recipe+ever): [White Cake](https://www.allrecipes.com/recipe/17481/simple-white-cake/)

Go check out the recipe linked, take a moment to visualize how you’d really move around the kitchen making it. Imagine this in YOUR kitchen being done by YOU — your utensils, your counters, your mixing bowls, your oven and your cleanup.

Do you see it? Are you really going to do your Mise En Place like the photo?

Mise en place (Source: <https://www.allrecipes.com/recipe/17481/simple-white-cake/>)

This is probably how you’ve seen all baking — in cookbooks, on TV, and so on. Do you keep a healthy stash of variously-sized glass or ceramic bowls? Do you look forwarding to cleaning them all?

If you measure ingredients out as you go, do you continually flip back to the proportions? Do you need to clean your measuring cups and spoons when the ingredients are incompatible, i.e. measuring out flour after the milk? It sounds exhausting, and it’s insane that the Big Chef industry promotes it.

The problem is…

#### Recipes don’t teach execution

Unless you’re a trained Chef, wherein “running a commercial kitchen” is part of the training, even cooking classes don’t do much to help.

Let’s rewrite that recipe just a bit differently:

1. Start with two 2 mixing bowls and a cheap kitchen scale. We’ll call them Bowl 1 and Bowl 2. If you have a stand mixer, Bowl 1 should be its bowl.
2. Turn oven onto 350F.
3. Place bowl 2 on the scale. Pour in 1 1/2 cup (or 188 g or 6.5 oz) of flour. Pour in 1 3/4th teaspoons (8 to 10 g) of baking powder. Mix gently.
4. Place Bowl 1 on weighing scale. Add 1/2 cup (1 stick in US or 227 g or 250ml) of butter. Pour in 1 cup (200g or 7oz) sugar.
5. Mix contents of bowl 1. If using stand mixing, start it off to do it’s thing. Once it’s slightly homogenized, add an egg. Once that egg is mixed in, add a second egg. Add vanilla. Once mixed thoroughly, using a spoon begin adding contents of Bowl 2 gradually as they homogenize.
6. Place bowl 2 back on weighting scale. Measure out 1/2 cups (or 120 g) of Milk.
7. Pour milk into Bowl 1. Mix.
8. Dump everything in a baking pan, and stick in the oven for 30–40 minutes.

Three things to observe here:

1. I’ve got all my measurements and conversions inline. I’m not looking at “Step 20: Add **the butter**” and going back 3 pages to figure out how much butter. Nor am I prepping in one of my infinite bowls before I begin.
2. When possible I don’t even waste a measuring cup or spoon. I pour flour, sugar, milk, and such straight out of their respective bags or cartons and using my measuring scale.
3. Even if you insist on using measuring cups — you’re measuring out the dry ingredients (sugar and flour) first, and then using the same cup for the milk. That’s it. One cup to clean up. And 2 bowls to clean up. If you’re using weights, just pour stuff in the bowls until you hit the target. Invest in a transparent measuring cup with lines on it, so you’re not using 3 separate ones for 1/4, 1/3, 1/2 or 1 cup.
4. I order instructions, so I only go forward. If I need separated egg whites and yolks, I add that as an instruction. If I’m going to need the whites the next day for an eggwash over my pastry, I write that as an instruction (and upfront mention I need a tupperware container.) I write down how long to wait for something to rest, or if I should be interleaving some other step in between (cleaning up, whipping cream or egg whites, soaking raisings in brandy, etc.)

Sorry if this is really basic, and yet I noticed repeatedly that participants came out surprised that all they had to clean were 2 spatulas, a couple of bowls, a stand mixer attachment, a whisk and a measuring cup.

Once you begin to rewrite recipes in an execution focussed manner, your cleanup grows logarithmic to your number of steps and ingredients. Simply put if you make a recipe that has ten times as many steps and ten times as many ingredients, you only clean up twice the number of utensils. If your recipe is a hundred times as much, you only clean up three times as much.

So now you know the secret to my constant baking. I can go 1000x more complex than a simple white cake, and I only have to clean up four times as much. That almost incentivizes me to go complex because I’m getting a 100x better product for only twice the effort.

Every cake, no matter how complex, has some dry stuff (sugar, flour), some wet stuff (water, fat)and in the most elaborate cases, a 3rd bowl for whipped egg whites. That’s it. Whether it’s a Genoise sponge, or a Red Velvet or a Chiffon or even the simplest pound cake — measure 1lb of everything, mix and boil (apparently the only method of cooking known to the british).

#### All your cooking friends implicitly do this

If you’ve been intimidated by that one excellent cook you know — I promise they’re intrinsically reordering steps to optimize execution and nobody is doing it the way a TV chef does.

#### Other random tips

1. Start with highest-temperature stuff towards lowest. If you want to toast peanuts or spices, do it on a dry pan — you need heat to get all those aromatics out. Then add oil and fry stuff that needs evenly-distributed high heat — browning your meats, getting char and flavor on vegetables, sauteeing onions. Finally you add anything with water — tomatoes, celery, bok choy. Once the temperature drops, your spices stop toasting and your peanuts get soggy. There’s a place for boiled peanuts in food, so be sure to know what you’re going for.
2. Try to avoid sudden temperature drops — buy a cheap electric kettle. If you need to add water to soups, stews, etc. Boil it before dumping it in. The cooking process abruptly stops when you drop temperature like that.
3. Baking is NOT precise. This random comment pisses me off everything I hear it and I suspect the Big Chef lobby has something to do with it. Your baking probably sucks because you’re measuring too precisely. The worst way to make french macarons, for instance, is to measure everything precisely and not think further. Not all almond flour has the same moister every day. Not all eggs are uniformly sized. Instead, you have to know what consistency you are looking for. Same for breads, cakes and what not. Your outcomes will be consistent if you know what intermediate consistencies should look like. The good recipes will describe them. This doesn’t make cooking more technical or complex. In fact, makes it easier.
4. This also applies to 90% of recipes you’ll come across, if they weren’t specifically designed for home proportions. Most recipes are scaled down from commercial kitchens where things sit around for a while — soups get reduced over a day, egg whites get dry if you’ve separated them in the morning, dough rests a lot. Instead focus on what consistency you’re trying to achieve and adjust it.
